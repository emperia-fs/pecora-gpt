The man

Born in Nicosia, Sicily in 1882, Ferdinand Pecora arrived in New York at age four. He put himself through law school while working as a law clerk, was admitted to the bar in 1911, and spent two decades as one of Manhattan's most tenacious assistant district attorneys.

In January 1933, with the Senate's investigation into the 1929 crash going nowhere, Pecora was hired as its fourth chief counsel. Within weeks, he had forced the resignation of the president of National City Bank and made the front page of every newspaper in the country.

Time magazine put him on its cover in June 1933. Roosevelt gave him carte blanche. The hearings that bore his name reshaped American capitalism.

The investigation

Pecora interrogated the most powerful financiers in America under oath: J.P. Morgan Jr., Charles E. Mitchell of National City Bank, Albert H. Wiggin of Chase, Richard Whitney of the New York Stock Exchange.

He exposed preferred stock lists given to the politically connected, short-selling schemes run by the very banks managing depositor funds, tax avoidance through Canadian shell companies, and securities sold to the public that insiders knew to be worthless.

The evidence was drawn from the banks' own records — subpoenaed, mastered overnight, and wielded in cross-examination with no notes.

The laws he built

  • Glass–Steagall Act, 1933 Separated commercial and investment banking. Prevented deposit-taking banks from gambling with customer funds in securities markets.
  • Securities Act, 1933 Required full disclosure before any securities could be offered to the public. Established civil and criminal penalties for fraud.
  • Securities Exchange Act, 1934 Created the SEC to police financial markets. Required ongoing public reporting from listed companies. Pecora was one of its first commissioners.
  • Federal Deposit Insurance, 1933 Created the FDIC, guaranteeing bank deposits and ending the bank runs that had wiped out ordinary savers.
